Minimal whiteboard Chinese explainer illustration style.

Visual DNA:
- Pure white background only. No gradients, shadows, texture, paper grain, realistic lighting, or complex environment.
- Thin black hand-drawn lines with a casual human feel. Lines should look lightly sketched by hand, not polished vector icons.
- Use a mix of simple stick figures and simple concept icons as the core visual language.
- Each image should explain one clear idea, one question, one contrast, one mechanism, or one tiny moment.
- Keep the composition sparse, readable, and easy to understand at a glance.
- Use large empty areas and simple spatial relationships: left/right contrast, before/after, one path, one obstacle, one handoff, one missing piece, one comparison, one timeline, or one cause-and-effect relationship.
- Use only a few short handwritten Chinese labels when needed, with occasional English keywords if helpful. Keep labels extremely short.
- Allow simple explanatory diagrams when useful, such as a timeline, comparison, storage metaphor, blocked path, memory container, branching choice, or a crossed-out wrong idea.
- The image should feel like a smart hand-drawn whiteboard explainer, not a cute cartoon or a formal infographic.

Characters and objects:
- Use simple stick figures when human action helps explain the idea.
- Default human characters are simple stick figures: round head, thin line body, thin line arms and legs, minimal understated face.
- A recurring character must keep the same template-level form across frames. Do not change age, species, clothing type, body type, or role when the prompt says it is the same entity.
- Also allow scenes centered on objects, symbols, diagrams, or metaphorical setups without a person.
- Recurring objects must keep the same recognizable silhouette, proportions, and key features across frames. Do not redesign a recurring object from frame to frame.
- Useful visual elements include: arrows, question marks, red crosses, check marks, clocks, calendars, brains, folders, boxes, maps, doors, locks, paths, simple charts, memory symbols, repeated blocks, and simple everyday props.
- Poses and object relationships should carry the meaning. Human faces should stay minimal and understated.

Entity consistency:
- When the prompt includes Visual Bible entity specs, treat them as hard consistency constraints.
- Render the same entity with the same identity, silhouette, proportions, and key details in every frame where it appears.
- Pose, angle, scale, and action may change, but the entity must remain recognizably the same.
- If the current scene does not need a listed recurring entity, do not force it into the image.
- Do not write internal entity ids, entity names, or labels such as "main_character" inside the image unless the scene explicitly asks for that visible text.

Color usage:
- Mostly black line art on white background.
- Use very limited accent colors only when helpful for emphasis.
- Red can indicate error, blockage, danger, or negation.
- Blue, orange, or green can be used sparingly for short labels or key emphasis.
- Do not turn the image into a colorful poster.

Communication style:
- The humor should be dry and understated.
- The meaning should come mainly from the drawing, not from text.
- One frame should communicate one point clearly and quickly.

Avoid:
- Cute children cartoon, emoji style, mascot poster, polished flat illustration, PPT infographic, dense text, realistic UI, screenshots, 3D, cinematic lighting, comic-book action drama.
- Do not write a big title in the image.
- Do not overload one frame with multiple arguments, too many props, or a long chain of reasoning.
- Do not explain everything in labels. The scene itself should carry the meaning.
- Do not make every frame depend on a person acting; some frames should work better as concept diagrams or metaphor scenes.
